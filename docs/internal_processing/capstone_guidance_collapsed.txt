Program Context 1.1 What Acies Global Global is Building Acies Global is building a suite of eight interactive Virtual Labs, collectively branded as the Acies Agentic Bus Virtual Labs. These are not slide decks or marketing videos. They are working, browser-based interactive demos that a VP-level business leader can sit in front of, explore, and walk away from with a clear understanding of: ● What the problem area looks like when it's working well (and when it isn't) ● How AI and analytics can improve decisions in that domain ● How Acies Global's agentic operating model — the Acies Agentic Bus — takes that further These labs are a core GTM (Go-To-Market) asset. They are designed to open conversations with Senior Director / VP-level leaders. 1.2 Who Uses These Labs The primary audience is functional VPs and senior leaders one or two levels below CXO — VP Supply Chain, VP Marketing, VP Operations, VP Finance Ops, VP Procurement, VP Logistics, VP Product, VP Customer Experience. The lab must feel immediately relevant to what they do every day. Secondary users are practitioners — the planners, analysts, managers, and leads who report to those VPs. They need a different depth and emphasis in tooltips and guided tours. 1.3 The Acies Agentic Bus Every lab is part of the Acies Agentic Bus brand. The Agentic Bus is Acies Global's framework for how AI-assisted decisions evolve into agentic, orchestrated operations over time. In the labs, the Agentic Bus appears as a storytelling artifact — a "How This Evolves" side panel that slides in from the right — not as the main UI. The main experience is always the interactive functional modules. --- Page 3 --- The consistent narrative across all labs: Now (fragmented, reactive, manual) → AI-Assisted (smarter decisions, faster signals) → Acies Global Agentic Bus (orchestrated, agent-led workflows) 2. Structured Problem Solving — Your Discovery Process 3.1 What is SPS? Structured Problem Solving (SPS) is a consulting methodology that helps you move from an ambiguous problem statement to a clear, evidence-backed solution. It is the same approach used in top consulting firms and is a core skill at Acies Global. Good SPS means: ● You understand the problem before jumping to a solution ● Your thinking is MECE — Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive ● You test hypotheses rather than assert opinions ● You use evidence to validate or invalidate assumptions ● Your recommendations are specific and actionable 3.2 SPS Steps for This Program Follow these steps in sequence. Do not begin building until you have completed Steps 1–4. Step 1: Frame the Problem Start with your lab's problem brief. Write a clear problem statement in this structure: " [Target user] struggles to [do what] because [root cause] , which results in [business impact] ." Then define success criteria (3–5 measurable outcomes), scope (in/out), and initial assumptions. Step 2: Build the Diagnostic Issue Tree An issue tree is a MECE breakdown of your problem. ● Diagnostic issue tree — "Why does this problem exist?" ● Solution issue tree — "How could this problem be solved?" Both are required. Issue trees must be 3+ levels deep, MECE at every level, with testable hypotheses at leaf nodes. --- Page 5 --- Step 3: Map the As-Is State Map the current business process as a swimlane or linear flow. Label each step with the responsible role. Identify where things go wrong — delays, errors, blind spots, manual workarounds. Step 4: Research and Validate Use stakeholder interviews (see Section 4) and secondary research to test your hypotheses. Document each hypothesis as Validated / Partial / Invalidated with evidence. Step 5: Define the To-Be State Define the improved process or workflow, the KPIs that improve, the role of AI, and the role of the Agentic Bus. This becomes your lab's solution concept. Step 6: Design the Lab Solution Translate the To-Be state into 5 lab modules. Each module should answer a specific question for a specific role. This becomes your module/tab map. 4. Stakeholder Interview Guidance 4.1 Who to Interview Each team should interview at least 3–5 people across: ● An LST member with experience in the relevant functional domain ● Account managers with the problem area ● An Acies Global consultant with relevant experience (analyst, planner, manager) ● An industry contact (optional) Use the Stakeholder Interview Template as a guide to good interviews. 4.2 Core Question Categories ● Problem framing: What is the biggest challenge? What decisions are hardest? ● Current state: How does the key process work today? What systems are used? ● Pain points: What would you change first? What workarounds exist? --- Page 6 --- ● Decision making: Who decides what? How far in advance are problems visible? ● Success criteria: What would 10x improvement look like? What KPIs matter? ● Data and technology: What data exists but isn't used well? What has been tried? 4.3 Interview Best Practices ● Prepare a 1-page guide before each session ● Summarize notes within 24 hours ● Ask "why" and "what does that mean in practice?" — surface-level answers are not enough ● Capture surprises — things that contradict your initial assumptions are the most valuable findings ● Document quotes; they are powerful in the SPS presentation 5. Key Frameworks Reference MECE: Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive. Every branch covers a distinct portion of the problem; together they cover all of it. As-Is / To-Be: Paired process flows. As-Is shows the current state with pain points. To-Be shows the future state with the solution. Hypothesis-Driven Problem Solving: Form a hypothesis early, seek evidence to validate or invalidate it, update as you learn. KPI Tree: Hierarchical map from top-line outcomes → process metrics → module-level indicators. Ensures modules connect to real business value.
